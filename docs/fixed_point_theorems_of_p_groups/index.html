Fixed-point Theorems for $p\ $-groups

A standard method to study a finite group is to first find its center; if non-trivial, we can set up an induction proof for certain existence theorems by quotienting by $Z(G)$.


  The results here, used in tandem with Cauchy’s Theorem, are the backbone for the proofs of the Sylow Theorems.

Non-triviality of $Z(G)$

This is indeed the case for finite $p$-groups, which we now show. Fix a prime $p$.

Proposition.If $G$ is a $p$-group acting on a finite set $S$, then $\l|Z\r|\equiv\l|S\r|\,\mod\,p$, where $Z$ is the set of fixed-points of this action.
Proof.
Click to expand...
Letting $A\subseteq S$ contain exactly the elements with non-trivial orbits, we have $$\begin{equation} \l|S\r|=\l|Z\r|+\sum_{a\in A}\l[G:G_a\r] \end{equation}$$ by the Class Equation. Since $G$ is a $p$-group, each summand $\l[G:G_a\r]$ is a power of $p$ greater than $1$, and hence is $0\,\mod\,p$.$\blacksquare$
Corollary.If $G$ is a $p$-group, the center $Z(G)$ is nontrivial.
Proof.
Click to expand...
Letting $G$ act on itself by conjugation, the above gives us $\l|Z(G)\r|\equiv\l|G\r|\,\mod\,p$. Since $\l|G\r|>1$ is a power of $p$, this shows that $\l|Z(G)\r|$ is a multiple of $p$. As $e\in Z(G)$, we see that $\l|Z(G)\r|\neq0$ and hence $\l|Z(G)\r|\geq p$.$\blacksquare$

Structure of $p$-subgroups

We now apply the previous results to understand the $p$-subgroups of a finite group $G$.


  Let $H\substructeq G$ of a $p$-subgroup of a finite group $G$.

Lemma.We have$^\dagger$ $[N_G(H):H]\equiv[G:H]\,\mod\,p$.
Proof.
Click to expand...

We are done if $H$ is trivial. Otherwise, $H$ acts on $G/H$ by left-multiplication, whose fixed-points are the cosets $gH$ such that $h(gH)=gH$ for all $h\in H$, which are those such that $H\subseteq g^{-1}Hg$ $-$ or equivalently, $g\in N_G(H)$.


  Thus, the fixed-points thereof are the cosets $gH$ of $H$ where $g\in N_G(H)$, of which there are $[N_G(H):H]$ many. Since $H$ is a $p$-group, we are done.$\blacksquare$

Proposition.If $H$ is not a $p$-Sylow subgroup of $G$, then there is a $p$-subgroup $K\substructeq G$ such that $H\nsubgrpeq K$ and $[K:H]=p$.
Proof.
Click to expand...

Since $H$ is not a $p$-Sylow subgroup, we see that $p$ divides $[G:H]$, and hence $p$ divides $[N_G(H):H]$ too. By Cauchy’s Theorem, there is an element $x\in N_G(H)/H$ of order $p$, which generates a cyclic subgroup $K_0\coloneqq\gen{x}$. Set $K\coloneqq\pi^{-1}(K_0)\substructeq G$.


  By the Lattice Isomorphism Theorem, we have $H\nsubgrpeq K$ and $$\begin{equation} K/H\iso\pi(K)/\pi(H)\iso K_0, \end{equation}$$ and so $[K:H]=|K_0|=p$ as desired.$\blacksquare$
