Integers

The set of integers $-$ denoted $\Z$ $-$ appear everywhere in algebra:

  • In group theory, it is the free group on one generator, and is used to construct all cyclic groups, and more generally, all finitely-generated abelian groups.
  • In ring theory, it is the initial object in $\catring$, and is the most important example of a PID. In fact, abelian groups are just $\Z$-modules.

The fundamental property of $\Z$, and that underlies all of its group and ring-theoretic properties, is the Division Algorithm.

Theorem (Division Algorithm for $\Z$). For any $a,b\in\Z$ with $b\neq0$, there exist unique $q,r\in\Z$ with $0\leq r<|b|$ such that $a=qb+r$.
Proof.
Click to expand...

We may assume $b>0$; otherwise, applying it to $-b$ furnishes unique $q’,r’\in\Z$ with $0\leq r’<-b$ such that $a=q’(-b)+r’$. Setting $q\coloneqq-q’$ and $r\coloneqq r’$ gives us the desired result. To this end, set $S\coloneqq\l\{a-kb\in\N\st k\in\Z\r\}$, which is non-empty since $$\begin{equation} 0\leq a+a^2b=a-(-a^2)b\in S. \end{equation}$$ By well-ordering of $\N$, we have a minimal element $r\in S$, giving us some $q\in\Z$ with $a-qb=r$. If $r\geq b$, then $0\leq r-b=a-(1+q)b\in S$ with $r-b<r$, a contradiction.


  For uniqueness, if $q’,r’\in\Z$ also satisfy the conclusions, then $qb+r=q’b+r’$, so that $r’-r=(q-q’)b$. Assume w.l.o.g. that $r’\geq r$, and thus $0\leq r’-r=(q-q’)b<b$. Cancelling $b$ gives us $0\leq q-q’<1$, forcing $q=q’$ and $r=r’$.$\blacksquare$

Corollary. $\Z$ is a Euclidean domain, and is hence$^\dagger$ a PID, hence$^\dagger$ a UFD.
Corollary. Subgroups of $\Z$ are precisely $n\Z$ for $n\geq0$.
Proof.
Click to expand...
Let $G\substruct\Z$. If non-trivial, it contains a minimal positive element $n\in G$, and we claim that $G=n\Z$. Clearly, ‘$\supseteq$’ holds. If $m\in G$, then $m=qn+r$ for $q,r\in\Z$ with $0\leq r<n$. Then $r=m-qn\in G$, which by minimality of $n$ forces $r=0$.$\blacksquare$
