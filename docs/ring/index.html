Ring

Motivation & Definition

The concept of a ring naturally arises when studying the endomorphism group of an abelian group $G$. Indeed, $\End G$ is itself abelian, but more importantly, it has an extra operation of composition. These operations are compatible in the sense below.

Definition. A ring is a tuple $(R,+,\cdot,0,1)$ consisting of a monoid $(R,\cdot,1)$ and an abelian group $(R,+,0)$, where the two structures are compatible in the following way: For all $r,s,t\in R$, we have the distributive laws $$\begin{equation} (r+s)\cdot t=r\cdot t+s\cdot t\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \mathit{and}\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ t\cdot(r+s)=t\cdot r+t\cdot s. \end{equation}$$

Remark. Note that although many rings arise as $\End(G)$ for some abelian group $G$, they do not exhaust all rings; a counterexample is the ring $\Z/2\Z\times\Z/2\Z$, or the family of rings $\F_{p^2}$ for $p$ prime (the latter are finite fields). See, however, Cayley’s Theorem for rings, which states that every ring $R$ is a subring of $\End(R)$, where $R$ is its underlying abelian group.

  The relevant structure-preserving maps for rings are as follows.

Definition. A function $f:R\to S$ between rings is a homomorphism if it is a homomorphism between groups that preserves identity [$f(1_R)=1_S$] and the multiplication operation [$f(r_1\cdot r_2)=f(r_1)\cdot f(r_2)$ for all $r_1,r_2\in R$].

Arithmetic in Rings

In this context, the operation $\cdot$ is often called multiplication (usually suppressed, so we write $rs$ for $r\cdot s$), which plays well with addition; for instance, we have $0r=r=r$ and $-r=(-1)r$ for all $r\in R$. However, ‘cancellation’ (i.e. $rs=rt$ implies $s=t$ for all $r\neq0$) does not hold in arbitrary rings. The issue is that there are elements $0\neq r\in R$ such that $rs=0$ for some $s\neq0$, which are called (left-)zero-divisors. Those are the only trouble makers, in that $r$ is a left-zero-divisor iff the left-multiplication map $s\mapsto rs$ fails to be injective.

Proof. Click to expand... If $r$ is a left-zero-divisor, then $0,s\mapsto rs=0$ for some $s\neq0$. If not, and $rs=rt$, then $r(s-t)=rs-rt=0$, so $s=t$.$\blacksquare$

Another point to beware of is that elements may not be (left)-units, which are elements $r\in R$ such that $rs=1$ for some $s\in R$. These elements test whether the left-multiplication map is surjective or not.

Proof. Click to expand... If $rs=1$ for some $s\in R$ and $t\in R$, then $r(st)=(rs)t=t$. If not, then $1$ is not in the image of this map.$\blacksquare$

Thus one should be careful in the arithmetic of rings, but those pitfalls will be eliminated in special types of rings (namely, integral domains and division rings, respectively).

The Category of Rings

With ring homomorphisms as morphisms, rings form a category $\catring$. This category is$^\dagger$ complete and cocomplete, as witnessed by the standard constructions of products, coproducts, kernels, cokernels, subobjects, and quotients. We also have a functor $\catring\to\catgrp$ taking a ring $R$ to its group of units, which is left-adjoint to the group ring construction.


  The zero-ring is clearly a final object. More importantly, the integers $\Z$ is an initial object.

Proof. Click to expand... Indeed, for every ring $R$ there is a group homomorphism $f:\Z\to R$ sending $n\mapsto n1_R$, which is a ring homomorphism since $$\begin{equation} f(nm)=(nm)1_R=n(m1_R)=(n1_R)(m1_R)=f(n)f(m) \end{equation}$$ by distributivity; it is unique by the requirement that $f(1)=1_R$ and $f$ preserves addition.

Types of Rings

Beyond the generalities above, much more can be said about specific types of rings.

  • Commutative rings are those whose multiplication is commutative, and they are studied in the field of commutative algebra. [In these notes, basically every ring is commutative, but we will always specify so.]
  • Integral domains are commutative rings with no non-zero zero-divisors. They abstract the properties of the integers, and they are stratified via their number-theoretic properties into UFDs, PIDs, and Euclidean domains.
  • Division rings are those in which every non-zero element is invertible. Wedderburn’s Little Theorem states that all finite division rings are commutative, so they are finite fields.
  • The most special rings are fields, which are commutative division rings, whose Galois theory kicked-off abstract algebra and led to the definition of groups.
