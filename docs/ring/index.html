Ring

Motivation & Definition

The concept of a ring naturally arises when studying the endomorphism group of an abelian group $G$. Indeed, $\End G$ is itself abelian $-$ but more importantly, it has an extra operation of composition. These operations are compatible in the sense that:

Definition. A ring is a monoid $(R,\cdot,1)$ equipped with the structure of an abelian group $(R,+,0)$ such that for all $r,s,t\in R$, we have $$\begin{equation} (r+s)\cdot t=r\cdot t+s\cdot t\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \mathit{and}\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ t\cdot(r+s)=t\cdot r+t\cdot s. \end{equation}$$

The relevant structure-preserving maps for rings are as follows.

Definition. A function $f:R\to S$ between rings is a homomorphism if it is a group homomorphism $f:(R,+)\to(S,+)$, such that $f(1_R)=1_S$ and $$\begin{equation} f(r_1\cdot r_2)=f(r_1)\cdot f(r_2) \end{equation}$$ for all $r_1,r_2\in R$.

Examples and Generalizations

Types of Rings

Beyond the generalities above, much more can be said about specific types of rings.

  • Commutative rings are those whose multiplication is commutative, and they are studied in the field of commutative algebra.
  • Integral domains are commutative rings with no non-zero zero-divisors. They abstract the properties of the integers, and they are stratified via their number-theoretic properties into UFDs, PIDs, and Euclidean domains.
  • Division rings are those in which every non-zero element is invertible.
  • The most special rings are fields, which are commutative division rings, whose Galois theory kicked-off abstract algebra and led to the definition of groups.
