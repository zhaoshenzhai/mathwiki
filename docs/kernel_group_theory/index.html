Kernel (Group Theory)

Motivation & Definition

A special property of the category of groups is the existence of a zero object; namely, the trivial group $\l\{0\r\}$; by definition, we have a morphism $0:G\to\l\{0\r\}\to H$ for each pair $G,H$ of groups. Now, for an arbitrary homomorphism $f:G\to H$, we would like to find some group $K$ that ‘forces’ $f$ to ‘equalize’ with $0$ in a universal way. This can be done in two $-$ dual $-$ ways, called the kernel and cokernel of $f$.

The first is to consider homomorphisms $\alpha:K\to G$ from some group $K$, and replace $f$ by $f\circ\alpha:K\to H$. One way to force $f\circ\alpha=0$ is accomplished by the following

Definition.

The kernel of a homomorphism $f:G\to H$ is the

subgroup $$\begin{equation} \ker f\coloneqq\l\{g\in G\st f(g)=e_H\r\}\substructeq G. \end{equation}$$

Clearly, $\iota:\ker f\to G$ composes with $f$ to the trivial map. It is moreover $^\dagger$ the ‘most efficient’ way of doing so, in the sense that it is the terminal object in the slice category $(K,\alpha:K\to G)$ making $f\circ\alpha=0$; this can then be interpreted as a colimit.

Normal Subgroups

Kernels of homomorphisms are very special subgroups, in that they correspond precisely to the normal ones.

  Indeed, if $f:G\to H$ is a homomorphism, then $g(\ker f)g^{-1}=\ker f$ for each $g\in G$ and so $\ker f\nsubgrpeq G$ is normal. Conversely, every normal subgroup $N\nsubgrpeq G$ is the kernel of the canonical projection $\pi:G\onto G/N$.

Cokernel

Dually, we consider maps $\beta:H\to K$ to some group $K$. The cokernel of $f$ is the initial object in the coslice category $(K,\beta:H\to K)$ making $\beta\circ f=0$. It can $^\dagger$ be realized as the canonical projection $\pi:H\to H/\gen{\im f}_N$, where $\gen{\slot}_N$ takes the normal closure, that is, the smallest normal subgroup containing $\im f$.


  However, $\l\langle\im f\r\rangle_N$ is not easy to describe at all, so the notion of a cokernel is not that useful in $\catgrp$. However, since $\l\langle\im f\r\rangle_N=\im f$ when $f:G\to H$ is a morphism in $\catabgrp$, we obtain a much more well-behaved cokernel.
