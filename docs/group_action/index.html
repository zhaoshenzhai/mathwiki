Group Action


Groups capture $-$ abstractly $-$ the notion of a symmetry. But symmetries are also present in concrete objects, with different kinds of objects preserving different kinds of structure. The concept of a group action connects the two.

Definition.

Let $G$ be a group and fix a

category $C$. A $G$-action is a

functor $\cat{B}G\to C$, which specifies an object $X\in C$ and a homomorphism $\phi:G\to\Aut X$.

Thus a group action is specified by the data of a tuple $\tpl{X,\phi}$, which we usually write as $\phi:G\act X$. The object $X$ is the concrete object, and $\phi$ encodes how $G$ manifests itself as $\Aut X$, the symmetries of $X$. Note, however, that $\phi$ need not be injective.

  Taking $C\coloneqq\catset$ recovers the usual notion of a group acting on a set, in which case $\Aut X$ are just the permutations of $X$. With $X$ as the underlying set of $G$, we have the canonical left-multiplication action given by $\phi(g)x\coloneqq g\cdot x$.

  In general, for a group action $\phi:G\act X$ with $X$ in a concrete category, we make the following definitions.

  • For a fixed $x\in X$, the orbit of $x$ is $Gx\coloneqq\l\{gx\in X\st g\in G\r\}$ and the stabilizer of $x$ is $G_x\coloneqq\l\{g\in G\st gx=x\r\}$. They are related by the Orbit Stabilizer Theorem and, usually when $G$ is finite, the Class Equation.
  • We say that the action is transitive if for all $x,y\in X$, there is some $g\in G$ such that $gx=y$. Note that $X\iso\bigsqcup_iGx_i$, and since $G$ always acts transitively on its orbits, every group action decomposes into its transitive components (i.e. orbits).
  • We say that the action is faithful if $\phi$ is injective, so that $G\into\Aut X$, and free if $gx=x$ for some $x\in X$ implies $g=e$. Cayley’s Theorem is the statement that the left-multiplication action is faithful.

Since group actions are functors, it makes sense to consider natural transformations between two actions $\phi:G\act X$ and $\psi:G\act Y$ $-$ called $G$-maps $-$ which defines the category of $G$-actions.
